Overview

This chapter covers the foundations of Technical Writing for ENG 103, including its definitions, differences from academic writing, main features, purposes, and common document types.

Study Beats Content

1. What is technical communication?

Definition: The exchange of information that helps people interact with technology, achieve workplace goals, and solve complex problems.

Examples: Cell phone instruction manuals, printer/microwave oven setup information, online courses, writing emails and reports, etc.

2. Technical Writing vs. Academic Writing
  • Technical Writing: Reader based, task oriented, and design based.
  • Academic Writing: Writer based, supporting the writer's stance, and relies on paragraph form.
3. Who creates technical communication?
  • All professionals function as technical communicators.
  • Anyone in a workplace setting can be a technical communicator.
  • Experts in any field are often required to present their knowledge to nonexpert audiences.
4. Main features of technical communication
  1. Focuses on the reader not the writer (user-centered communication).
  2. Is efficient and accessible.
  3. Is clear and relevant.
  4. Uses media effectively.
  5. Is created by both individuals and teams.
  6. Targets a global audience.
  7. Is persuasive and truthful.
  8. Is based on research.
5. Three purposes of technical communication
  • Informational purpose: Anticipate and answer questions.
  • Instructional purpose: Help people to perform a task.
  • Persuasive purpose: Encourage readers to take an action.
6. Common types of technical documents
  • Memos
  • Emails
  • Letters
  • Instructions
  • Procedures
  • Manuals
  • Brochures
  • Proposals
  • Reports
1. Importance of Proofreading

Basic errors will distract the reader and make the writer look careless.

2. Effective Strategies
  • Save it for the final draft: Don't proofread while drafting.
  • Take a break: Rest before proofreading.
  • Work from hard copy: Spot mistakes on paper.
  • Proofread multiple times: Read more than once.
  • Don't rely only on AI: Never rely completely on Grammarly.
1. Thinking Critically About Research

Critical thinking means testing the quality of information and accuracy of interpretations. Instead of accepting information at face value, you should examine, evaluate, verify, analyze, and weigh alternatives.

Effective Strategies: Ask the right questions, explore a balance of views, explore topic in depth, evaluate sources, and interpret findings objectively.

2. Primary Vs. Secondary Sources
  • Primary Research: Getting information directly from the source (e.g., conducting interviews, surveys, observing people or events).
  • Secondary Research: Getting information second-hand by reading compiled works (e.g., books, articles, websites).
3. Hard-Copy Vs. Web-Based Sources

Hard-Copy Sources:

  • Benefits: Found on shelves with related materials, easy to determine author/date, easier to preserve and keep secure.
  • Drawbacks: Time-consuming to search, offer only text/images, hard to update.

Web-Based Sources:

  • Benefits: More current and accessible, searches can be narrowed/broadened easily, offers material with no hard-copy equivalent.
  • Drawbacks: Might only access recent material, not always reliable, user might get confused by too many choices.
4. Strategies for Internet Research
  • Select technical keywords rather than general ones.
  • Identify the site's purpose and sponsor.
  • Assess the currency of the site and its materials.
  • Assess the author's credentials and assertions.
  • Download only what you need, use it ethically, and cite sources.
5. Categories of Hard-Copy Reference Works
  • Bibliographies
  • Indexes
  • Encyclopedias
  • Dictionaries
  • Handbooks
  • Almanacs
  • Directories
  • Abstracts
  • Gray Literature
1. Providing Usable Information

This chapter focuses on providing audiences with usable information by considering the 6 essential steps when writing a technical document.

1. What Does Ethics Mean?
  • Ethical questions often revolve around technology.
  • Ethical stance requires a personal decision.
  • Ethical decisions are not always black and white.
  • Your ethical decisions do affect the users, your company, your society, and your job.
2. Types of Ethical Choices
  • Medical technologies: (e.g., genetic testing) raise questions about personal privacy & medical insurance.
  • Banking & retail operations: (collecting personal info) raise concerns about how this information is used and who has access to it.
  • Environmental pollutants: (e.g., pesticides or smokestacks) raise serious questions about the health of the planet.
3. Why Does Ethics Matter?

Unethical decisions can harm a company's reputation as well as workers and customers.

Example: When an instruction manual is written & vital safety information is purposely omitted to save on the cost of printing, people can be injured.

4. Examples of Unethical Communication
  • Plagiarizing work of others
  • Falsifying information
  • Suppressing information
  • Exaggerating claims
  • Using images that conceal truth
  • Stealing proprietary info
  • Misusing electronic info
  • Exploiting cultural differences
5. Strategies for Avoiding Ethical Abuses
  • Always cite your sources if the information is not your own.
  • Give the audience everything it needs to know and a clear understanding.
  • Never manipulate information or data in your writing or visuals.
  • Use common sense and follow company confidentiality guidelines.
  • Do not exploit cultural inequalities.
  • Ask yourself: "Would I stand behind what I have created if held publicly accountable for it?"
1. Structure vs. Non-Standard Structure

Structure organizes information so readers can understand it.

  • Standard Structure: Intro (attracts attention), Body (supports viewpoint), Conclusion (reemphasizes key points). Used in reports, proposals, letters.
  • Non-standard Structure: Uses columns, colors, and images. Used in brochures, manuals.
2. Outlining

Guides the reader point by point. A formal outline undergoes changes as you write.

  • Alphanumeric Notation: Uses Roman numerals, capital letters, Arabic numerals, and lowercase letters.
  • Decimal Notation: Uses Arabic numerals (e.g., 2.0, 2.1, 2.1.1).
3. Chunking

Breaks information down into smaller units to show how ideas are connected.

  • Web sites: Information is expected in very short chunks.
  • Printed page: Readers expect longer passages.
4. Sequencing

Shows the logical progression of information:

  • Spatial: How parts fit together (physical objects).
  • Chronological: In what order things happen (events).
  • Problem-solving: Diagnosis to solution.
  • Cause & Effect: Describes an incident then traces causes.
5. Paragraphing & Headings
  • Topic sentence: Represents the main idea.
  • Unity: Each sentence expands on the topic.
  • Coherence: Connected line of thought. Damaged by wrong order or lack of transitions.
  • Headings: Break up long passages (not all documents need them).
  • Overview: Brief summary explaining the purpose and what to expect.
1. Structure vs. Style
  • Structure: The reader's ability to understand the document as a whole.
  • Style: Readability at the sentence level (construction, length, tone, word choice).
2. Writing Clearly

Avoid these to maintain clear writing:

  • Ambiguous pronoun references.
  • Ambiguous modifiers (only, just, almost, never).
  • Passive voice (unless appropriate, like "The data were analyzed").
  • Nominalizations (using nouns instead of verbs).
  • Stacked modifying nouns (too many nouns in a row).
  • Unnecessary jargon.
3. Writing Concisely
  • Avoid Wordiness: Replace long phrases with single words (e.g., "due to the fact that" -> "because").
  • Eliminate Redundancy: Remove repetitive pairs (e.g., "completely eliminate", "end result").
4. Writing Fluently
  • Combine related ideas: A series of short, choppy sentences loses interest.
  • Use parallel structure: Ensure grammatical similarity (e.g., running, kayaking, and designing).
5. Writing Personably (Friendly Tone)
  • Adjust tone: Choose formal, semiformal, or informal. Address readers as "you". Use "I" and "we". Emphasize the positive.
  • Avoid sexist/biased language: Use gender-neutral expressions (e.g., chairperson). Use person-first language for disabilities ("person with a disability"). Use preferred cultural designations.
1. Written Communication Types

The three common types of written communication in the workplace are:

  • Memos
  • Letters
  • Emails
2. Memos vs. Emails
  • Memos: Turned into PDF files/attached to emails, leave a paper trail (printed), and are formal. They give directives, provide instructions, make requests, and are easy to post.
  • Emails: Can function like a memo, leave a digital trail (electronic), and are less formal.
1. Instructions & Procedures Strategies
  1. Analyze your audience and purpose.
  2. Always remember ethical and legal implications.
  3. Use standard organization and needed elements.
  4. Provide appropriate level of detail and technicality.
  5. Write with a readable style.
  6. Design for maximum accessibility.
  7. Always test for usability.
2. Usability Testing

With instructions and procedures, accuracy and ease of use are important. Conduct a basic usability survey and ask: "Do these instructions and procedures carry out the task safely, efficiently, and accurately?"

1. Importance & Purposes of Definitions

Importance: Explain specialized terms to people without expertise in the field.

Purposes: Level of language should match the audience's background. Clear definitions help the public understand complex technical issues and protect the writer legally and ethically.

2. Types of Definitions
  • Parenthetical: Clarify meaning using a familiar synonym in parenthesis.
  • Sentence definition: Uses the "term-class-features" method for concepts needing elaboration.
  • Expanded definition: Short paragraph or several pages using methods like etymology, history, or visuals.
3. Methods for Expansion & Placement

Methods: Etymology, history, negation, operating principle, analysis of parts, visuals, comparison, and examples.

Placement: Brief definitions in parenthesis; sentence definitions in running text; expanded definitions near the beginning or in an appendix.

1. Importance & When to Use Visuals

Visuals help readers interpret and remember complex information. They should enhance a document, support text, or make a point more clearly than text alone.

2. Tables, Graphs, and Charts
  • Tables: Numeric (quantitative) and Textual (qualitative).
  • Graphs: Bar graphs (changes over time, patterns) and Line graphs (large quantities of data).
  • Charts: Flowcharts (processes), Pie charts, and Organizational charts.
3. Diagrams, Icons & Symbols
  • Diagrams: Exploded diagrams (separated parts) and Cutaway diagrams (interior view).
  • Icons: Resemble the items they represent (e.g., a file folder).
  • Symbols: Abstract; get the meaning across without resembling the item (e.g., biohazard sign).
1. Importance of Document Design

Design refers to the layout of words and graphics, determining the document's look. It affects usability, guides readers, and helps them understand the information easily.

2. Characteristics of Well-Designed Documents
  • Inviting (attractive) and accessible (easily to get to).
  • Flow well as a single cohesive unit.
  • Provide visual hierarchy (displaying information in the right order).
1. What is a Summary?

A summary is a restatement of the specific details or examples in a longer document. It conveys the meaning of the original source's ideas using the same tone, but it is always shorter than the original document.

2. Elements of an Effective Summary
  • Accuracy: Do not change facts.
  • Completeness: Include all important information.
  • Conciseness: Exclude unnecessary details.
  • Nontechnical style: Use simple English.
1. Special Types of Summaries
  • Closing Summary: Appears in the concluding section to help readers review and remember major findings.
  • Informative Abstract: Precedes the full report. Condenses the issue, methods, findings, and conclusion.
  • Descriptive Abstract: Appears on a separate page. States what a document covers without detailed information to help people decide whether to read it.
  • Executive Summary: Precedes the full report. Helps guide the thinking of decision makers.
2. Ethical Considerations & Strategies

Ethical Issues: Summaries might fail to communicate full complexity or distort the original writer's intent and tone.

Strategies: Read the original, highlight essential info, rewrite in your own words (to avoid plagiarism), edit, check against original, and indicate the exact source.

1. What is a Résumé?

A résumé is an applicant's personal advertisement for employment. It lists education, employment history, and other relevant information to give an employer an instant overview of your qualifications.

2. Résumé Organization Types
  • Standard (Reverse chronological) résumé: Lists the most recent school and job first.
  • Functional résumé: Highlights skills relevant to a particular job.
3. Résumé Parts & Strategies

Parts: Contact information, Career objectives, Education, Work experience, Personal data/interests, and References.

Strategies: Tailor for each job, limit to a single page, stick to relevant experience, use action verbs, and never invent or distort credentials.

1. Application Letters & Portfolios

Application Letters (Cover Letters): Explain how qualifications fit a job. Solicited (for advertised jobs) and Unsolicited (for unadvertised jobs).

Dossiers & Portfolios: Provide proof of credentials (transcripts, recommendation letters, examples of work).

2. Interview Strategies
  • Confirm time and location. Bring a briefcase, pen, notepad, and extra résumés.
  • Make a positive first impression and dress properly.
  • Don't answer with just yes/no, and if you don't know an answer, say so.
  • Never criticize a previous employer.
3. Follow-Up Letters

Always follow up after an interview. The two main types are: Thank you letters (sent after the interview) and Acceptance or Refusal letters (sent after receiving a job offer).

1. What is a Proposal?

A proposal is an offer to do something or a suggestion for action. Its main purpose is to persuade the reader to adopt your plan, buy your product, or hire your services.

2. Solicited vs. Unsolicited Proposals
  • Solicited: Requested by an organization (often via an RFP - Request for Proposal).
  • Unsolicited: Initiated by the writer without a specific request, trying to convince the reader that a problem exists and needs a solution.
1. Formal Reports

Formal reports are highly structured documents used to solve complex problems, present research, or provide extensive data. They are usually longer and more formal than memos or letters.

2. Parts of a Formal Report
  • Front Matter: Title page, transmittal letter, table of contents, informative/descriptive abstracts.
  • Body: Introduction, methods, results/findings, conclusion, and recommendations.
  • Back Matter: References, glossary, and appendices.
1. Emails (Short Written Messages)

Emails leave a digital trail and are generally less formal than printed memos, but should still maintain a professional tone in the workplace.

2. Oral & Sales Presentations

Delivery Styles:

  • Memorized: Avoid in workplace settings.
  • Extemporaneous: The preferred style. Uses an outline but provides room to interact with listeners.
  • Scripted: Read verbatim.
  • Impromptu: Natural connection, no script.

Overcoming Anxiety: Be prepared, dress for success, take charge, and gesture naturally.

3. Corporate Blogs & Social Networks
  • Internal Corporate Blogs: Help employees network, replace long emails, deliver training, and enhance workflow.
  • External Corporate Blogs: Communicate with customers, facilitate feedback, enhance marketing, and show a personalized side of the company.
